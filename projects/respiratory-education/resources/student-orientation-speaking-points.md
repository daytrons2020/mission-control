# New Student Orientation — Speaking Points
## Education Hospital Guidelines & Expectations

---

## Opening (5 minutes)

**Welcome & Introduction**
- Welcome to [Hospital Name] — thank you for choosing us for your clinical education
- Brief personal introduction: your role, experience, how long you've been here
- "This orientation will set you up for success during your time with us"

**Icebreaker Question**
- "Before we dive in — what's one thing you're most excited to learn here?"
- Or: "What's your biggest question about clinical rotations?"

---

## Part 1: Hospital Overview (10 minutes)

**Our Mission & Values**
- Patient safety is our #1 priority — always
- Commitment to education: we exist to train the next generation
- Interdisciplinary teamwork: RTs, nurses, doctors, techs — we work together

**What Makes Us an "Education Hospital"**
- Teaching happens at the bedside, not just in classrooms
- Expect to be asked questions — it's how we learn
- Mistakes are learning opportunities (if you own them and fix them)
- Research and evidence-based practice drive our protocols

**Hospital Layout & Key Areas**
- ICU locations, step-down units, general floors
- Where to find: break rooms, lockers, parking, cafeteria
- Emergency equipment locations on each unit

---

## Part 2: Professional Expectations (15 minutes)

**Attendance & Punctuality**
- Arrive 15 minutes early for your shift — no exceptions
- Call-in procedure: who to call, how early, what information to provide
- "If you're on time, you're late. If you're early, you're on time."

**Professional Appearance**
- Uniform requirements: scrubs, ID badge, clean professional look
- Personal hygiene: patients are close to you — be mindful
- No strong perfumes/colognes (patients have respiratory issues!)
- Shoes: closed-toe, non-slip, comfortable for 12-hour shifts

**Communication Standards**
- SBAR when giving report: Situation, Background, Assessment, Recommendation
- Read-back protocol for verbal orders
- Always introduce yourself to patients: name, role, purpose
- Use teach-back method: "Can you tell me in your own words what we discussed?"

**Electronic Devices**
- Phones on silent/vibrate — never in patient rooms
- No personal calls/texts at the nurses' station
- Hospital computers: clinical use only, HIPAA applies

---

## Part 3: Clinical Expectations (20 minutes)

**Scope of Practice — Know Your Limits**
- "If you haven't been checked off on it, you don't do it alone"
- Always work under supervision of your preceptor
- Ask before acting — better to ask than harm a patient
- Graduated responsibility: you'll do more as you prove competency

**Patient Safety Non-Negotiables**
- Hand hygiene: before and after EVERY patient contact
- Two patient identifiers before any procedure
- Check allergies before giving meds or treatments
- Fall precautions: bed alarms, call lights within reach
- Never ignore alarms — investigate immediately

**Documentation**
- "If it wasn't charted, it didn't happen"
- Real-time documentation when possible
- Be objective, factual, complete
- Never chart for someone else
- Ask for help if the EMR is confusing

**Infection Control**
- Standard precautions for every patient
- Contact/droplet/airborne precautions — know the signs
- PPE: don and doff properly (we'll demonstrate)
- Isolation rooms: check before entering

---

## Part 4: Learning Culture (15 minutes)

**Questions Are Encouraged**
- "The only dumb question is the one you don't ask"
- If you're wondering, ask — curiosity shows engagement
- Keep a notebook: write down questions for later

**Feedback Is a Gift**
- You'll receive feedback regularly — it's not personal
- How to receive feedback: listen, don't defend, ask clarifying questions
- How to give feedback: specific, constructive, timely

**Teaching Moments**
- Bedside teaching happens constantly
- "See one, do one, teach one" — when you're ready
- Case presentations: you'll present patients to the team
- Journal clubs: participate, learn evidence-based practice

**Mistakes & Near Misses**
- Everyone makes mistakes — what matters is how you handle them
- Immediate disclosure to your preceptor
- Patient safety event reports: not punitive, for learning
- "Covering up a mistake is worse than the mistake itself"

---

## Part 5: Support & Resources (10 minutes)

**Your Preceptor**
- Primary point of contact for clinical questions
- Schedule regular check-ins (daily or per shift)
- Preceptor evaluations: mid-rotation and final

**Educational Resources**
- Simulation lab: practice skills in a safe environment
- Library access: journals, textbooks, online resources
- Skills checklists: know what's expected

**Student Support Services**
- Counseling/mental health resources
- Academic support if struggling
- HR for any workplace concerns

**Who to Call for Help**
- Clinical questions: your preceptor
- Emergency: Rapid Response or Code Blue
- After-hours concerns: charge nurse or supervisor

---

## Part 6: Policies & Procedures (10 minutes)

**HIPAA & Patient Privacy**
- Never discuss patients in public areas (elevators, cafeteria)
- No photos of patients, charts, or computer screens
- Social media: zero tolerance for patient information sharing
- "Would you want your medical story shared?"

**Social Media Guidelines**
- Never post about specific patients or situations
- No photos in patient care areas
- When in doubt, don't post it
- Violations can end your rotation and career

**Substance Use Policy**
- Zero tolerance for impairment on duty
- Random drug screening
- If you have a problem, seek help before it finds you

**Sexual Harassment & Professional Boundaries**
- Report any inappropriate behavior immediately
- Maintain professional relationships with all staff
- Patients are vulnerable — never exploit that

---

## Closing (5 minutes)

**Questions & Discussion**
- "What questions do you have?"
- "What's your biggest concern about starting?"

**Final Encouragement**
- "You're here because we believe you can do this"
- "This will be challenging — growth always is"
- "We're here to support your success"

**Next Steps**
- Badge photo/activation
- EMR training session schedule
- First day with preceptor: when and where to report
- Contact information exchange

## Speaking Tips for Presenter

1. **Make eye contact** — engage with students personally
2. **Use stories** — real examples stick better than rules
3. **Pause for questions** — don't rush through
4. **Be approachable** — set the tone for their experience
5. **End on time** — respect their schedule
6. **Provide handouts** — they can't remember everything

*Adjust timing based on your hospital's specific needs and policies.*
